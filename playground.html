Brief

I received the Oslo tourism app brief in August 2020, a time when Oslo was effectively closed to foreign tourists and access to sites of interest was significantly restricted due to pandemic guidelines. This left me with two possible directions. I could base the concept on usersâ€™ experiences before the pandemic, with a view to make an app that would become valid again a soon as restrictions lifted. Or I could confine my research to the challenges of present moment and hope that this extreme situation would create unique insights that could prove useful also when the pandemic restrictions lifted.

I chose the latter, to make it a â€˜domestic tourist during a pandemicâ€™ project, and set about interviewing Oslo based friends, family, and colleagues over the phone, asking them about their current urban leisure options. During much of 2020 this was essentially confined to going for walks or hang out in parks and public squares.

User interviews

Through research I discovered several sources of frustration across different demographics, but most interestingly I found that parents of young children could spend quite a bit of time searching for suitable playgrounds. As playgrounds can be owned and maintained by a wide range of different organisations, there is no central registry in which they can be found, and more importantly from whom information about them can be channelled to services such as Google or Four Square. That doesnâ€™t mean that playgrounds are impossible to find, after all there are plenty of them around, but itâ€™s difficult to know in advance if they are any good. According to my research, the best ways a parent could approach this matter would be to make a visual assessment from Google Mapsâ€™ satellite view or street view or, more commonly, network with other parents. This networking could be directly, through for example WhatsApp groups, or indirectly by searching online and discovering blog posts or social media comments.

Starting with a rather wide demographic I narrowed down my project by basing the app on the subgroup whose need was the most poorly served by what was currently available.

Concept

This project seeks to help parents of young children by designing an app built upon a map indexed registry of good playgrounds â€“ a registry that will be structured around the usersâ€™ needs. It aims to let parents exchange experiences through a review and rating system and encourages them to enrich the information available through submitting their own photographs. Lastly, I have added an editorial dimension, where articles and copy have the potential to enhance engagement as well as potentially opening avenues to monetize the project.

Sketching

Following the research and concept development stages, the project moved to a pen and paper stage. The first set of drawings were rather crude sketches of the appâ€™s main screens. These were then loaded into the Marvel app for testing, allowing me to work out the most basic information flows and seek out immediate user feedback.

For the second and third iterations of the project I moved the sketching routine to dot paper and took care to consider the full length of the scrollable app screen.

Sketching the app's layout and information flow made me realise (very slowly I must admit) how profoundly different the UI of an app is from that of a web page.

Lo-fi wireframes

Where the concept and sketching phases had uncovered an abundance of ideas for what the app could be the lo-fi wireframes presented some harder decisions in terms of what categories of information would be given priority, in what order the information ought to be presented, and what should be cut out entirely. The first two iterations were user tested with the Adobe XD app, but by the third iteration the Figma Mirror app had improved to the point where it could support impromptu user testing using a phone only.

Flowchart and navigation

My initial concept was quick and straightforward, where the app would open directly in map view allowing the user to immediately see which playgrounds were within walking distance from their present location. However, most tourism and sightseeing apps are based on a central core of editorialised content, allowing the user to plan their itineraries well in advance. Later iterations therefore explored the scope of what this content could be and how this could be made helpful to the target demographic.

Stylistic explorations

When working on the mid-fi wireframes I started using real data, while also playing around with various layouts and stylistic directions I had been wanting to try out. This introduced discipline to the process. The need to convey information from real playgrounds would regularly present challenges to my design ideas, compelling me to quickly throw out any sources of inspiration that could not be applied in practice.

Then followed a more intensive phase as I developed the feedback from the mid-fi wireframes into a hi-fi prototype with all relevant screens and functionality. Running frequent iterations, I tested the app on an ongoing basis with just a couple of dedicated users.

Early iterations of the visual design took inspiration from the playground itself, a site of energy and bold colours, but through user testing the design gradually changed towards simplicity and ease of use.

Design system

Quicksandâ€™s open shapes and square geometry loosens up the density of information, while the rounded terminals subtly reinforce the family friendly spirit of the app.

Text and icons are presented in strong contrasts, black or anthracite on white or beige backgrounds. Yellow and coral serve as accents and add focus to the navigation experience, presented in slightly sun bleached hues to create an outdoorsy feel.

User testing favoured the more conventional icon designs, rendered as open shapes with thin outlines.

WCAG compatibility was checked with Figma plugins.

Towards the end of the visual design phase I created a more systematic spacing structure and immediately saw improvements to the overall feel of the app.
